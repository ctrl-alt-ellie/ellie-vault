Cipher Assignment

In senior year of highschool, I got to make a custom cipher for my cybersecurity class. It was a substitution cipher, and initially I think that's all I started with--symbols that corresponded to letters. But then I noticed the fact that we could add our own custom rules, and so right away I started cooking up a horrendous cipher. I believe it was the frequencies of letters converted to base 64 and then grouped into threes after translation. An example of this would be "The quick brown fox", which would become "OS4 xNi 4xM Tiu Nw= =MC 4xM i44 Ny4 wMi 44M C43 Nw= =MS 41N i4w Ny4 1Mi 40N i43 Mi4 yNy 41M C4x NQ= =". It was awful and cursed, and I loved it. The person who had to use it did not. This is what kickstarted my first conlang, because I enjoyed making the cipher so much that I decided to take on a bigger challenge.

O'eaiā

O'eaiā is a Polynesian/Chinese inspired conlang that popped up in my Youtube recommended. I believe it was made as a submission to a "cursed conlang" competition, CCC3 was the name I think. They are currently doing voting for that, but I'm considering submitting something for CCC4. Anywho, the first time I watched the video, I was absolutely hyped--genuinely giddy with excitement. That video is really good and I think ZeWei (the artist) is making another one this January. They even made documentation and everything! It tickles the probable 'tism in my head. It also gave me the motivation to do a complete overhaul on the language I was working on at the time, Ictuk.

Hoolotan

Hoolotan was my first attempt at creating a language. It took structures from English and French, as those were the languages I knew/were learning at the time. It also had a limit: every single word had to start with the letter 'h', no matter what. The pronunciations created from this were entertaining to say the least, some said it sounded like you were laughing the whole time, which I can't argue.

Numbers

Multiplier Word

In Hoolotan, larger numbers are represented through a multiplier of 5. Anything after 5 can be added into.
Structure:
(5) + Hê (hɛ) + (Number being multiplied) + (Remainder) Example (55): Héuhêhéuhéuhé

In Hoolotan, “he (hɛ)” is pronounced the same way as “hê (hɛ)”, so the ê is more of a distinguisher for the multiplier word.
Mixed Example (47, 5 * 9 + 3): Héuhêhéuhéohée

1 or a/an Hé (heɪ) 6 Héuhé (heɪu:heɪ)
2 Héa (heɪʌ) 7 Héuhéa (heɪu:heɪʌ)
3 Hée (heɪɛ) 8 Héuhée (heɪu:heɪɛ)
4 Héo (heɪoʊ) 9 Héuhéo (heɪu:heɪoʊ)
5 Héu (heɪuː) 10 Héuhéu (heɪu:heɪu:)

Verb Rules

Verb Suffixes

-ah (ɑ) General non-compound verbs.
-ih (ɪ) Most commonly used in loan and composite verbs. Also the most common verb suffix.
-eh (ɛ) Always irregular but happen to have the most important verbs
